Briefing Notes - Prepared by Mohammed Ahmed

Purpose: easyMind is a mental health support application designed specifically for university students aged 18-25. It addresses rising concerns about student anxiety, stress, and emotional fatigue by providing three core features: AI-guided journaling for emotional expression and personalized suggestions, integrated peer and counselor chat support for real-time help, and mood tracking visualization to monitor mental health patterns over time.

Background: Many existing mental health apps fail to serve student needs effectively due to overwhelming interfaces, lack of personalization, and absence of integrated support systems. easyMind solves these problems through a minimalist design approach (maximum 3 taps to reach any feature), AI-powered personalized responses, and seamless connection to both peer mentors and professional counselors within the university ecosystem.

Observations - Prepared by Mohammed Ahmed

Task 1: AI-Guided Journaling Observations

Alice (University Student): Alice immediately identified and clicked the Journal button on the homepage, demonstrating the effectiveness of our clear visual hierarchy. She hesitated briefly before typing personal content, expressing concern about privacy. The AI response impressed her with its relevance and personalization. She particularly appreciated the practical suggestions and actually attempted the breathing exercise. Task completion time: 3 minutes.

Dr. Lopez (Campus Counselor): Dr. Lopez approached the journaling feature from a clinical perspective, immediately evaluating the appropriateness of AI-generated mental health guidance. He appreciated the supportive tone but questioned the depth of analysis. He specifically mentioned wanting integration with counselor dashboards and student monitoring systems. Task completion time: 4 minutes.

John (Peer Mentor): John tested the journaling feature by putting himself in his mentees' shoes, demonstrating his empathetic approach to peer support. He praised the welcoming, non-intimidating interface design. The AI's suggestion to connect with peer support aligned perfectly with his mentoring philosophy. Task completion time: 3.5 minutes.

Task 2: Chat Support Access Observations

Alice (University Student): Alice immediately gravitated toward peer support chat over counselor options, finding it less intimidating. She appreciated seeing the number of active members (3) as it felt manageable rather than overwhelming. The anonymity features provided comfort and confidence. Task completion time: 2 minutes.

Dr. Lopez (Campus Counselor): Dr. Lopez immediately focused on counselor features but found them insufficient for professional needs. He required dashboard functionality, student request queues, priority flagging for urgent cases, and appointment scheduling integration. Task completion time: 3 minutes with extensive suggestions.

John (Peer Mentor): John explored both peer and counselor chat options to understand the complete support ecosystem. He appreciated the real-time activity indicators and different chat categories. However, he identified critical needs for group moderation tools and the ability to flag serious issues to counselors. Task completion time: 2.5 minutes.

Task 3: Mood Tracking Observations

Alice (University Student): Alice found the mood chart immediately intuitive and engaging. She could correlate specific days with events. The color-coded weekly overview format resonated with her preference for visual information. She successfully logged her current mood using the emoji system but requested more granular tracking capabilities. Task completion time: 1.5 minutes.

Dr. Lopez (Campus Counselor): Dr. Lopez evaluated the mood tracking from both student usability and professional utility perspectives. He appreciated the clear visualization and simple emoji system but immediately raised concerns about student data privacy and consent protocols. Task completion time: 2 minutes.

John (Peer Mentor): John focused on how mood tracking could enhance his group mentoring effectiveness. He understood the individual visualization clearly but wanted aggregated group mood trends to help him plan community activities. Task completion time: 2 minutes.

Findings - Prepared by Harin Shorsh

Usability Problems Discovered

Problem 1: Privacy Transparency Gap

Issue: Users expressed uncertainty about data sharing protocols between students, peer mentors, and counselors

Evidence: All three users specifically asked about privacy during testing

Severity: High - directly affects user trust and system adoption

Solution: Implement a comprehensive privacy dashboard with granular control settings and transparent explanations of who can access what information.

Problem 2: Role-Based Access Confusion

Issue: Users experienced confusion about different chat room purposes and access levels

Evidence: Alice attempted to access counselor-only features while Dr. Lopez was unclear about peer chat protocols

Severity: Medium - causes navigation errors and potential misuse of features

Solution: Implement role-based color coding, clear access level indicators with tooltips, and personalized onboarding tutorials.

Problem 3: Limited Mood Context

Issue: Mood logging lacks contextual information about why mood changed

Evidence: All users requested the ability to add notes or tags to mood entries

Severity: Medium - reduces the analytical value of mood data

Solution: Add optional mood context tags (academic stress, social issues, health concerns) and brief note fields for mood entries.

Problem 4: Missing Crisis Support

Issue: No clear pathway exists for urgent mental health situations

Evidence: Dr. Lopez specifically highlighted this as a critical missing feature

Severity: Critical - represents a significant safety concern

Solution: Implement a prominent "Need Help Now" button accessible from all screens with 24/7 crisis hotline resources.

Success Metrics Achieved

  • Task Completion: All users completed core tasks under 5 minutes with minimal guidance
  • Navigation Intuitiveness: 100% of users found homepage navigation immediately clear and logical
  • AI Effectiveness: All participants rated AI suggestions as "helpful" and "personalized"
  • Visual Design: Unanimous positive feedback on calming aesthetics and reduced cognitive load
  • Technical Stability: Zero technical issues encountered during testing sessions
  • Core Concept Validation: All user personas confirmed the value proposition

Conclusion

Our Project 4 prototyping and evaluation successfully validated the easyMind concept while identifying specific areas for improvement. The high-fidelity prototype demonstrated that our user-centered design approach effectively addresses the core problems identified in existing mental health applications.

The usability testing with representatives from all three target user groups provided valuable insights into both the strengths of our current design and the enhancements needed for successful implementation. The positive response to our core features confirms that easyMind addresses genuine user needs in the university mental health support ecosystem.

Moving forward, implementing the identified solutions for privacy transparency, crisis support protocols, and enhanced professional features will transform easyMind from a promising prototype into a comprehensive mental health support system ready for campus deployment.
